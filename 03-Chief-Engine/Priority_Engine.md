# Priority Engine v1.0

## Purpose

This document defines how North Vector determines what deserves attention now, what can wait, what should be ignored, and what must be escalated.

The Priority Engine converts goals, deadlines, risks, opportunities, commitments, and constraints into an ordered set of actions.

## Core Principle

Everything cannot come first.

Priority is the disciplined allocation of limited time, energy, attention, and resources.

## Constitutional Authority

The Priority Engine must operate under the Constitution.

When tradeoffs occur, the constitutional hierarchy governs:

1. Physical safety
2. Physical health
3. Mental health
4. Legal obligations
5. Academic performance
6. Medical school competitiveness
7. Career development
8. Future optionality
9. Financial stability
10. Professional reputation
11. Family relationships
12. Important friendships
13. Mentorship relationships
14. Personal projects
15. Entrepreneurship
16. Exploration
17. Entertainment
18. Convenience
19. Short-term pleasure

This hierarchy is a default, not a crude automatic rule. Context still matters.

## Priority Inputs

The engine should evaluate:
- constitutional tier
- importance
- urgency
- downside risk
- opportunity value
- goal impact
- deadline proximity
- reversibility
- dependency impact
- effort required
- available time
- available energy
- behavioral risk
- relationship impact
- health impact

## Priority Score

A conceptual priority score may be calculated as:

Priority Score =
Constitutional Weight
+ Importance
+ Urgency
+ Risk Impact
+ Goal Impact
+ Opportunity Value
+ Dependency Impact
+ Behavioral Risk Adjustment
- Deferrability
- Resource Cost

The exact numerical weights should be tested and adjusted over time.

## Importance

Importance asks:

How much does this affect Nishad's meaningful goals or well-being?

High-importance examples:
- major exam preparation
- medical appointment
- legal deadline
- important mentor commitment

Low-importance examples:
- minor interface customization
- optional entertainment

## Urgency

Urgency asks:

How soon must action occur before value is lost or damage increases?

Urgency should rise when:
- deadlines approach
- opportunities expire
- consequences become harder to reverse
- dependencies block other work

Urgency should not automatically outrank importance.

## Risk Impact

Questions:
- What happens if this is ignored?
- How likely is the consequence?
- How severe is it?
- How reversible is it?

High-risk items should receive a priority boost.

## Opportunity Value

Questions:
- What could be gained?
- Does this create leverage?
- Does this expand optionality?
- Does the opportunity expire?

The engine should consider upside, not only threats.

## Goal Impact

An item should receive a higher score when it directly supports:
- a critical active goal
- multiple important goals
- a blocked parent goal
- a long-term strategic objective

## Dependency Impact

Tasks that unblock many other tasks should receive additional priority.

Example:

Complete course registration
↓
Unlocks schedule planning
↓
Unlocks work availability
↓
Unlocks study planning

## Behavioral Adjustment

Known failure modes should alter priority.

Example:

Known pattern:
Nishad underestimates chemistry workload.

Current situation:
Chemistry exam in eight days.

Adjustment:
Increase preparation priority earlier than a generic system would.

## Energy and Capacity

Priority should not be confused with scheduling.

A high-priority task may still need to be placed when energy and time are sufficient.

The engine should consider:
- cognitive demand
- emotional demand
- physical demand
- available focus
- recovery needs

## Priority Categories

### Critical

Requires immediate attention or protection.

Examples:
- safety issue
- health emergency
- exam tomorrow with inadequate preparation
- legal deadline today

### High

Should receive attention within the next few days.

Examples:
- approaching major deadline
- mentor follow-up
- important application

### Medium

Valuable and should be scheduled, but can wait briefly.

Examples:
- project development
- skill building
- routine relationship maintenance

### Low

Can wait without meaningful damage.

Examples:
- minor optimization
- optional browsing
- cosmetic improvements

### Ignore or Remove

Does not justify attention relative to current priorities.

## Dynamic Priority

Priority must update as circumstances change.

Example:

Exam in 30 days:
Medium

Exam in 7 days with adequate preparation:
High

Exam in 2 days with preparation behind:
Critical

## Daily Priority Queue

Chief should identify:
- one primary priority
- up to two secondary priorities
- necessary maintenance tasks

The system should resist generating an overwhelming list of equally urgent items.

## Weekly Priority Queue

Chief should identify:
- three to five major weekly outcomes
- major risks
- major opportunities
- protected health and relationship commitments

## Conflict Resolution

When priorities conflict, Chief should compare:
- constitutional tier
- consequence severity
- deadline
- reversibility
- opportunity cost
- relationship impact
- compromise options

Example:

Party Tonight
vs
Chemistry Exam in Three Days

Possible result:
- decline
- attend briefly
- attend only after study target is met

The engine should seek balanced solutions when they remain aligned.

## Priority Overrides

Priority may be temporarily overridden when:
- safety or health changes suddenly
- new legal obligations appear
- a high-value opportunity expires soon
- a major dependency becomes blocked
- the user intentionally changes strategy

Overrides should be visible and explained.

## Priority Decay

Some items lose priority when:
- deadlines pass
- opportunities expire
- goals change
- risks are resolved
- dependencies disappear

Completed items should leave the active queue quickly.

## Priority Escalation

An item should escalate when:
- time remaining shrinks
- progress remains below target
- consequences increase
- repeated postponement occurs
- failure modes become active
- a dependency blocks other important work

## Priority De-Escalation

An item may de-escalate when:
- progress improves
- risk is mitigated
- deadline moves
- opportunity value decreases
- the item is delegated or completed

## Transparency Requirements

Chief should be able to explain:
- why an item is prioritized
- which goals it affects
- what risk it prevents
- why another item can wait
- what assumptions were used

## Example Priority Assessment

Item:
Prepare for CHEM 1127Q exam.

Assessment:
- constitutional tier: academics
- importance: high
- urgency: high
- risk: high
- goal impact: high
- behavioral adjustment: elevated due to workload underestimation pattern

Result:
High to Critical priority depending on preparation status.

## Failure Modes

### Urgency Trap

Everything urgent becomes important.

### Over-Prioritization

Too many items are labeled high priority.

### Goal Blindness

Long-term goals are ignored because no deadline exists.

### Opportunity Blindness

Only risks receive attention.

### Behavioral Blindness

Historical patterns are ignored.

### Health Sacrifice

Academic or project priorities repeatedly displace sleep and recovery.

### Static Queue

Priorities fail to update as circumstances change.

## Phase 1 Implementation

Phase 1 should support:
- manual and rule-based priority scores
- constitutional tier mapping
- importance and urgency
- deadline proximity
- risk and goal links
- behavioral adjustments
- daily top-three selection
- transparent explanations

Machine-learned prioritization should come later.

## Success Criteria

The Priority Engine succeeds if Chief can reliably answer:
- What matters most right now?
- What should happen today?
- What can wait?
- What is becoming dangerous?
- What opportunity is about to expire?
- Why is this ranked above that?

## Final Principle

A Chief of Staff's first job is not to create more tasks.

It is to protect attention from the wrong tasks.

The Priority Engine exists to decide what truly comes first.
