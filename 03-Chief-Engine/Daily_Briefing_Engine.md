# Daily Briefing Engine v1.0

## Purpose

This document defines how North Vector generates daily operational briefings.

The Daily Briefing Engine transforms memory, goals, priorities, events, risks, opportunities, and commitments into a concise picture of what matters today.

Its purpose is to answer a simple question:

What does Nishad need to know right now?

## Core Principle

A daily briefing should create clarity, not information overload.

The goal is not to summarize everything.

The goal is to surface the few things most likely to affect the day.

## Mission

The Daily Briefing Engine should:
- establish situational awareness
- reinforce priorities
- surface risks early
- highlight opportunities
- maintain continuity
- reduce forgotten commitments
- align daily action with long-term goals

## Inputs

The briefing should draw from:
- Constitution
- Mission Brief
- active goals
- current priorities
- calendar events
- deadlines
- accountability records
- active risks
- active opportunities
- behavioral patterns
- relationship commitments
- recent reflections
- short-term memory

## Briefing Frequency

### Morning Briefing

Primary daily briefing.

Purpose:
Set direction for the day.

### Midday Briefing

Optional.

Purpose:
Course correction.

### Evening Briefing

Optional.

Purpose:
Reflection and preparation.

## Morning Briefing Structure

### 1. Executive Summary

A short overview of the day.

Example:

`Today is academically focused. Your chemistry preparation remains the highest-leverage activity. No major emergencies are present, but exam preparation risk remains elevated.`

### 2. Top Priorities

Identify:
- one primary priority
- up to two secondary priorities

Example:

Primary:
- Complete chemistry review target.

Secondary:
- Follow up with mentor.
- Finalize project milestone.

## 3. Schedule Overview

List:
- classes
- work shifts
- appointments
- meetings
- travel
- major commitments

Only relevant events should be included.

## 4. Active Risks

Surface:
- highest-impact risks
- escalation level
- recommended mitigation

Example:

`Orange academic risk: chemistry preparation remains behind schedule.`

## 5. Active Opportunities

Surface:
- expiring opportunities
- high-value openings
- strategic actions worth taking

Example:

`Research assistant application closes in four days.`

## 6. Relationship Commitments

Include:
- follow-ups
- promises
- important outreach
- family obligations

Example:

`Send orientation update to Dr. Chan.`

## 7. Behavioral Watchlist

Highlight relevant patterns.

Example:

`Watch for planning replacing execution. The next valuable step is implementation, not additional architecture.`

## 8. Health and Recovery

Review:
- sleep
- workload density
- recovery needs
- exercise opportunities

Health receives elevated constitutional protection.

## 9. Strategic Alignment

Connect today's work to long-term goals.

Example:

`Today's chemistry review supports freshman GPA goals, which support medical school competitiveness.`

## 10. Next Action

Identify the single most important next action.

Example:

`Complete a 45-minute chemistry diagnostic session before noon.`

## Midday Briefing Structure

Purpose:
Evaluate execution.

Questions:
- What has been completed?
- What remains?
- What has changed?
- Has risk increased?
- Should priorities be adjusted?

## Evening Briefing Structure

Purpose:
Close the day.

Questions:
- What was completed?
- What was missed?
- What should carry forward?
- What lesson emerged?
- What is tomorrow's primary priority?

## Priority Rules

The briefing should emphasize:

1. Safety
2. Health
3. Critical deadlines
4. Active risks
5. High-priority goals
6. Important commitments
7. Valuable opportunities
8. General information

## Brevity Rules

The briefing should:
- fit within a few minutes of reading
- avoid unnecessary detail
- emphasize action
- avoid repeating unchanged information excessively

A daily briefing is not a report.

It is an operational update.

## Personalization

The briefing should adapt to:
- current goals
- active projects
- academic schedule
- workload
- behavioral patterns
- preferred level of detail

## Escalation Integration

The Daily Briefing Engine should incorporate escalation levels.

Examples:

Level 1:
Mention in briefing.

Level 3:
Highlight clearly.

Level 5:
Place near the top and recommend immediate action.

## Opportunity Integration

The briefing should not become purely defensive.

Every briefing should consider:
- opportunities worth pursuing
- relationships worth strengthening
- strategic moves worth making

## Continuity Function

The briefing should preserve continuity across days.

Questions:
- What carried over?
- What changed?
- What remains unresolved?

Chief should remember unfinished stories.

## Failure Modes

### Information Dump

Too much detail.

### Generic Briefing

No personalization.

### Risk Obsession

Only threats are surfaced.

### Opportunity Blindness

Only maintenance work appears.

### Constant Repetition

The same message appears every day without adjustment.

### No Clear Action

The briefing informs but does not guide.

## Phase 1 Implementation

Phase 1 should support:
- daily morning briefing generation
- top priorities
- schedule summary
- active risks
- active opportunities
- relationship reminders
- behavioral watchlist
- next-action recommendation
- concise formatting

Advanced adaptive briefing styles can come later.

## Success Criteria

The Daily Briefing Engine succeeds if Chief can reliably answer:
- What matters today?
- What should happen first?
- What could go wrong?
- What opportunity should not be missed?
- What commitment requires attention?
- How does today connect to long-term goals?

## Final Principle

A Chief of Staff begins the day with situational awareness.

The Daily Briefing Engine exists to provide that awareness before the day begins.
