# Escalation Engine v1.0

## Purpose

This document defines how North Vector increases the visibility, urgency, and intervention level of important issues when passive monitoring is no longer sufficient.

The Escalation Engine exists to ensure that critical risks, neglected commitments, approaching deadlines, and significant opportunities receive attention proportional to their importance.

## Core Principle

Not every issue deserves interruption.

Some issues deserve monitoring.

Some deserve reminders.

Some deserve immediate action.

Escalation is the process of determining which is which.

## Mission

The Escalation Engine should:
- detect worsening situations
- increase attention proportionally
- prevent avoidable failures
- reduce alert fatigue
- coordinate with other engines
- preserve user autonomy

## Inputs

The Escalation Engine receives signals from:
- Priority Engine
- Risk Engine
- Accountability Engine
- Planning Engine
- Opportunity Engine
- Event Memory
- Goal Memory
- Behavioral Memory
- Relationship Memory

## Escalation Levels

### Level 0 — Monitor

No action required.

The issue is tracked passively.

### Level 1 — Awareness

The issue appears in briefings and dashboards.

No interruption is required.

### Level 2 — Prompt

A direct reminder or recommendation is surfaced.

Action is encouraged but not urgent.

### Level 3 — Strong Prompt

Chief explicitly recommends intervention.

The issue is becoming consequential.

### Level 4 — Warning

Consequences are likely without action.

Chief should clearly explain risks and next steps.

### Level 5 — Critical Intervention

Immediate attention is recommended.

Reserved for safety, health, legal, severe academic, or major strategic threats.

## Escalation Factors

### Time Sensitivity

How soon must action occur?

### Consequence Severity

What happens if nothing changes?

### Probability

How likely is the negative outcome?

### Reversibility

Can damage be undone?

### Goal Impact

Which important goals are affected?

### Relationship Impact

Will trust, reputation, or obligations be affected?

### Behavioral Risk

Does the situation match a known failure mode?

### Opportunity Expiration

Will the upside disappear if action is delayed?

## Escalation Formula

Conceptually:

Escalation Pressure =
Urgency
+ Severity
+ Probability
+ Goal Impact
+ Behavioral Risk
+ Opportunity Expiration
- Mitigation Progress

Exact weighting may evolve over time.

## Escalation Persistence

Escalation should not remain permanently elevated.

When progress occurs:
- reduce escalation
- update status
- remove unnecessary reminders

The goal is resolution, not permanent pressure.

## Alert Fatigue Protection

Too many warnings reduce effectiveness.

The Escalation Engine should:
- suppress duplicates
- combine related alerts
- prioritize highest-impact issues
- avoid repeating identical messages
- limit simultaneous critical alerts

## Behavioral Escalation

Known failure modes should accelerate escalation.

Example:

Pattern:
Repeated postponement of vague tasks.

Current situation:
Important project milestone delayed three times.

Response:
Escalate earlier than normal and require a concrete next action.

## Relationship Escalation

The engine should elevate issues involving:
- broken promises
- overdue responses
- neglected important relationships
- reputation-sensitive commitments

Trust is difficult to rebuild once lost.

## Health Escalation

Health receives elevated constitutional protection.

Examples:
- sustained sleep deprivation
- signs of burnout
- neglected medical concerns

Health-related escalation may outrank productivity concerns.

## Override Conditions

The engine may override normal prioritization when:
- safety is threatened
- health is threatened
- legal obligations are at risk
- severe irreversible consequences are possible

Overrides should be explicit and explainable.

## Escalation Resolution

An escalated issue should be reduced when:
- action is completed
- mitigation succeeds
- risk decreases
- deadline changes
- opportunity expires or is captured
- new information changes the assessment

## Escalation Communication

A useful escalation should answer:
- What is happening?
- Why does it matter?
- What changed?
- What action is recommended?
- How urgent is it?
- What happens if nothing is done?

## Example Escalation Message

`Level 4 Academic Warning: CHEM 1127Q exam preparation is behind schedule with four days remaining. Two planned review sessions have not been completed, and this situation matches a known workload-underestimation pattern. Recommended action: protect a 90-minute review block tonight and postpone lower-priority project work.`

## Failure Modes

### Chronic Alarm

Everything becomes urgent.

### Escalation Delay

Warnings arrive too late.

### Duplicate Alerts

The same issue is surfaced repeatedly.

### Weak Escalation

The system notices risk but fails to change behavior.

### Overriding Autonomy

Chief attempts to force decisions.

### Missed Opportunity Escalation

Opportunities expire without notice.

## Phase 1 Implementation

Phase 1 should support:
- escalation levels
- rule-based triggers
- links to risks, goals, events, and commitments
- duplicate suppression
- resolution tracking
- explanation generation
- health and safety overrides

Advanced adaptive escalation can come later.

## Success Criteria

The Escalation Engine succeeds if Chief can reliably answer:
- What deserves attention right now?
- Why is it escalating?
- What happens if nothing changes?
- What action should be taken?
- Is this warning proportionate?
- When can escalation be reduced?

## Final Principle

A warning is valuable only if it arrives early enough to matter.

The Escalation Engine exists to ensure that important signals are heard before they become consequences.
